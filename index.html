Where It Started

The Evolution of Travel Technology

From GDS to My Travel Data Cube  ·  Published on Iterative Livings, 2025

On a flight from Singapore to LAX in 2023, I watched a plane full of exhausted travelers fumble with apps trying to book ground transportation after midnight. Each one solving the same problem alone. I sketched a concept on a napkin: what if one trusted data layer knew your preferences, your loyalty status, your calendar, and could act on your behalf? I called it My Travel Data Cube.

The article traces my career from witnessing my parents book a trip at Hunt Travel in Poughkeepsie in the 1980s, to working Continental Airlines reservation lines, to helping build the PSS systems used by 60+ airlines at Navitaire. It maps every wave of travel technology and makes the case that the next one is not a better search interface — it is the elimination of search entirely.

The article generated inbound conversations from industry colleagues and a European venture capital investor. It also answered its own question: if the technology was finally ready, why hadn't someone built this? That question became Veyant.

The Work

Veyant  —  AI Orchestration for Corporate Travel

Active project, Oct 2025–Feb 2026  ·  Paused; not abandoned

Every travel platform makes users search. Veyant was built to eliminate search entirely — to give AI agents the infrastructure they need to not just find options, but act on them.

The Problem

Two structural barriers block AI in travel

The Trust Barrier: travelers will not share data with platforms that have conflicts of interest. The Capability Barrier: nobody has built the connectivity layer to actually transact across suppliers with full policy and loyalty context.

Result: 83% of TMC tickets handled manually at $20 each. 45-minute booking battles. Zero personalization.

The Concept

Three-Cube Architecture + AI Agents

My Travel Cube (traveler data, loyalty, preferences), Corporate Cube (policies, rates, approvals), Supplier Cube (NDC/GDS connectivity). Five AI agents working in parallel to take a natural language request to confirmed booking.

From $25/trip in 1985, to $10 in 2000, to $5 in 2020, to $2 in the AI era. The economics of getting this right are significant.

Five months of active work produced original frameworks, working architecture, a product backlog, four wireframed interfaces, a working demo built with Claude Code, and investor materials grounded in primary market research with TMC operators. The pitch deck tells the full story.
