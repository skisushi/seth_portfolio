The Evolution of Travel Technology

From GDS to My Travel Data Cube  ·  Published on Iterative Livings, 2025

On a flight from Singapore to LAX in 2023, I watched a plane full of exhausted travelers fumble with apps trying to book ground transportation after midnight. Each one solving the same problem alone. I sketched a concept on a napkin: what if one trusted data layer knew your preferences, your loyalty status, your calendar, and could act on your behalf? I called it My Travel Data Cube.

The article traces my career from witnessing my parents book a trip at Hunt Travel in Poughkeepsie in the 1980s, to working Continental Airlines reservation lines, to helping build the PSS systems used by 60+ airlines at Navitaire. The article generated inbound conversations from industry colleagues and a European venture capital investor. It also answered its own question: if the technology was finally ready, why hadn't someone built this? That question became Veyant.

The Work

Veyant  —  AI Orchestration for Corporate Travel

Independent research & proof-of-concept, Oct 2024–Feb 2026

Every travel platform makes users search. Veyant was built to explore a different question: what if AI agents had the context and infrastructure to act on a traveler's behalf — not just find options, but book, rebook, and service entire trips?

There are two things blocking AI in travel right now. First, the trust problem — travelers won't hand over their data to platforms that turn around and use it for marketing and dynamic pricing. The AI agent is blind. Second, the capability problem — nobody has built the connectivity to actually book, change, and service trips across suppliers with full loyalty and policy context. The AI agent can't execute. The travel robot is stuck.

Veyant's concept: three data cubes (traveler, corporate, supplier) feeding AI agents that work in parallel — from natural language request to confirmed booking. The traveler controls their data. The AI has the context to act. Result: 83% of TMC tickets currently handled manually could be automated.

Five months of independent work produced original frameworks, working architecture, a product backlog, four wireframed interfaces, and a working demo built with Claude Code. The pitch deck, the technical exploration, and the working code are all available below.

Product Design

Corporate Policy Manager

Design Wireframe  ·  Not yet built

Travel policy today is written in documents and enforced manually. The Corporate Policy Manager flips this — instead of writing rules, travel managers describe the business outcome they want, and the system derives the enforceable rules from that intent. A manager types "keep our United partnership healthy until we hit our Q4 volume target" and the system generates route preferences, fare delta thresholds, and volume alerts automatically.

Nine features are annotated in the wireframe: natural language policy input, objective-vs-rule mode, AI-generated derived rules, smart suggestions when rigid rules are entered, real-time compliance analytics, and conflict detection when policies contradict each other.

① Policy Categories

Sidebar navigation across Air, Hotel, Ground, Approvals, Preferred Suppliers, and Compliance — each with its own rule set and compliance tracking.

② Natural Language Input

Travel managers describe the business outcome they want in plain English. The system interprets intent and derives enforceable rules automatically.

③ Objective vs Rule Mode

Toggle between high-level business intent and granular rule editing — managers can stay in objective mode or go deep when needed.

④ Starter Templates

Pre-populated best-practice policies by industry and company size. Adopt as-is, edit to fit, or ignore entirely.

⑤ Objective Display

Every rule shows the business intent behind it in natural language — so new managers can understand why a policy exists, not just what it says.

⑥ Derived Rules

AI-generated enforceable rules with specific parameters — editable by the manager if the generated defaults don't match their intent.

⑦ AI Suggestions Panel

When a manager enters an overly rigid rule, the system surfaces smarter alternatives — reducing exception volume before policies go live.

⑧ Policy Insights

Real-time compliance rates, savings tracking, and exception patterns — so managers can see whether their policies are working or creating friction.

⑨ Conflict Detection

Alerts when two policies contradict each other — e.g. a preferred supplier rule that conflicts with a cost cap — before they cause booking failures.

The check_policy_compliance tool in the MCP server below is the runtime enforcement layer for policies configured here — deterministic, auditable, and separate from the AI reasoning layer.

Working Code

Veyant MCP Server  —  Proof of Concept

TypeScript / Node.js / Model Context Protocol  ·  GitHub →

Beyond the demo UI, I built a working MCP (Model Context Protocol) server that gives Claude structured access to three enterprise travel capabilities as callable tools: supplier search, traveler preference scoring, and corporate policy compliance.

The Tools

Three structured capabilities

search_travel_options — query flights and hotels by route and city.

get_preference_score — score any option 0–100 against the traveler's profile (carrier, seat type, loyalty, change fees).

check_policy_compliance — validate a proposed booking and return an explicit pass, exception, or violation verdict with per-rule detail.

The Key Design Decision

Policy in a dedicated system, not a prompt

The compliance tool is deterministic and auditable. In this demo it runs as a TypeScript rule engine. In production it would be replaced by an ML model trained on real booking and approval history — capturing the implicit policy that lives in years of decisions, not just the written rulebook. Claude receives a structured verdict either way.

The interesting case: Delta's business cabin normally violates Acme Corp policy (economy and premium economy only approved). But the traveler used loyalty miles for the upgrade — not a fare upgrade. Same cabin, different compliance outcome. The tool handles that distinction explicitly.

Live MCP output in Claude — preference scoring, policy compliance, and actionable recommendations

Three-Cube Data Architecture

All travel context organized into three domains — Personal (loyalty, preferences, credentials, trip history), Corporate (policies, negotiated rates, approvals, department budgets, YTD spend), and Supplier (inventory, availability, pricing, product attributes, descriptors). The MCP server proof-of-concept implements all three.

Model Selection Framework

Not every task needs an LLM. A four-tier hierarchy — regex and rules, local ML classifiers, small LLMs, large LLMs — matches capability to task complexity. At 100K bookings/month, tiered model selection reduces inference costs by ~65%.

Guardrails as Infrastructure

Policy enforcement through structured, auditable tools — not prompt training. Three layers: hard pre-decision constraints, soft preference-based scoring, and post-decision compliance validation. The MCP compliance tool is a working example of this pattern.

Data Security & Zero-Trust

Three-tier classification (credentials in HSM, PII encrypted, analytics anonymized). AI agents never touch credentials directly — a token vending service issues short-lived, scoped access tokens per transaction. Both corporate and traveler maintain control over their data with full visibility: access audit logs and purpose telemetry show exactly who accessed what, when, and why.
